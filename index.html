Below are some of the projects I am currently working on and some that I have already completed on my scientific journey. Here I find myself at the beginning of this journey, excitedly awaiting future projects around the next corner....

In the beginning, my work was mainly concerned with theoretical game theory. The mathematical intensity of this pursuit proved valuable for my later data-driven and programming-intensive projects. The initial steps of my journey were conducted in MatLab, including a program that solves games for Nash equilibria (see "Homotopy Methods for solving Non-Degenerate Bimatrix Games"), as well as a replication of an existing empirical paper using the same data set but a different programming language, namely Python (see "The Effects of Walth Shocks on Health"). If you value elegant and efficient programming, I strongly advise you not to check out these projects, since going through the code could cause you physical pain. Fortunately, this does not apply to more recent projects.

On a more serious note, I believe that as Data Scientists we are often very well educated in econometrics, but understand too little of our everyday data analysis tools. This dangerous half-knowledge has led me to take a step back and learn more about the deep-rooted fundamentals of computer science and the programming in C. To that end, I enrolled in several CS50x courses from HarvardX, which I expect to complete in 2022. Keep posted, I am eagerly looking forward to making the final project available here (see "HavardX CS50x (Soon Available)").

In fact, I would like to believe that these courses and my ignited passion for style and efficiency, are already paying dividends in more recent projects. One of these projects involves the data analysis underlying my working paper published in early 2022 (see "Developing a Framework for Real-Time Trading in a Laboratory Financial Market"). The analysis is performed in Python and features more sophisticated data engineering rather than complex models. Using a laboratory experiment, we collect financial market data from algorithmic trading at millisecond speed.

The latest projects, on the other hand, are less exciting in terms of data engineering, but they involve much more sophisticated models. The first project, "A Horse Race to classify Amazon Kindle Reviews", examines 1,000,000 reviews from the Amazon Kindle Book Store and conducts a horse race between different supervised machine learning models seeking to classify whether a previously unseen book review is positive or not. The second project, "Emojis as Labels & ROC-AUC vs. Average Precision", uses emojis from tweets to label positive or negative emotions and to train an Extremely Randomized Trees model. The trained model is used to classify the emotions of previously unseen, unlabeled tweets. In this way, it is possible to observe the change of emotions in Spain following the police brutality during the Catalan freedom movement in 2017. The focus of this project is to compare performance in terms of ROC-AUC vs. Average Precision. The third project, "Mountain Car & Neural Networks (Soon Available)", addresses a classical benchmark problem for reinforcement learning algorithms. Using neural networks, this project attempts to solve Mountain Car, a multi-dimensional problem for which the environment is provided by the gym module.
